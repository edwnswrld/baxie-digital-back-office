# Meet Your Back Office Crew

You run the jobsite. Your agents run the office.

Baxie's Digital Back Office is a small crew of digital employees that handle the
paperwork side of your business: estimates, schedules, change orders, invoices, and
reminders. You hire a real contractor for the hammer work. The crew handles the
desk work, and they never sleep, never lose a number, and never forget to chase a
signature.

Think of it like a 10-person office staff working for your 2-person shop. Here's who
they are, what they do, and just as important, what they will not touch.

---

## The team rules (every employee follows these)

Before you meet them one by one, here are the house rules that keep this crew
trustworthy:

- **Each person stays in their lane.** The Estimator prices work. The Scheduler
  sequences work. Nobody freelances into a job that isn't theirs.
- **Out-of-scope asks go back to the Office Manager.** If you ask the Estimator to
  reschedule the framing crew, they don't guess. They hand it to the Office Manager,
  who routes it to the Scheduler. You never have to know who does what.
- **They only see the job in front of them.** Each employee works on the current
  job's data and nothing else. They can't peek at another client's numbers, and they
  can't pull in outside files. One job, one set of eyes.
- **You sign every check.** The crew prepares the paperwork. The owner (you, or the
  homeowner on a change order) is always the one who approves it. Nothing goes out
  the door with money attached until a human says yes.
- **They check their own work.** Two of them grade their math and their sequencing
  against hard rules before anything reaches you. If they catch a mistake, they fix
  it and tell you they fixed it. No silent errors.

---

## The five employees

### 1. The Office Manager: your single point of contact

**Name:** Sam

**What Sam does:** Sam is the only person on the crew you talk to. You bring Sam a
request in plain English ("the homeowner wants to add an egress window in the
kitchen") and Sam figures out who needs to handle it, hands it off, and brings the
finished work back to you. Sam runs the office floor: assigning work, keeping the
hand-offs moving, and reporting status.

**What Sam won't do:** Sam doesn't price the work, build the schedule, or write the
invoice. That's not the job. Sam is the dispatcher and the front desk, not the
specialist. If a request needs real estimating or scheduling, Sam routes it to the
right person rather than winging it.

**How work flows:** Everything starts and ends with Sam. You talk to Sam, Sam talks
to the crew, the crew talks back to Sam, and Sam reports to you.

---

### 2. The Estimator: prices the work

**Name:** Maya

**What Maya does:** Maya turns a scope of work into a number. Hand her a list of what
needs to get built (a window opening, the framing, the flashing, the drywall patch,
trim, paint) and she puts labor hours, labor rate, and material cost on every line,
then adds your markup. The result is a clean, itemized price you can stand behind.

**What Maya won't do:** Maya doesn't decide *when* the work happens (that's the
Scheduler) and she doesn't write the change order or invoice (that's the Office
Admin). She prices, and only prices. If you ask her about timing, she sends it back
to Sam.

**How she checks herself:** Before Maya's number leaves her desk, it gets graded
against a hard rule book (the "estimate oracle"). If she accidentally counts the
framing labor twice or her line items don't add up to her total, the grader flags
it, Maya repairs the math, and re-checks. You only see the corrected number, plus a
note that she caught and fixed it.

---

### 3. The Scheduler: sequences the work (pre-construction)

**Name:** Theo

**What Theo does:** Theo builds the calendar. He takes the priced scope and lays out
the tasks in the right order, with start days, durations, and which crew has to
finish before the next one can start. He works the front end of the job:
pre-construction planning, before the dust flies.

**What Theo won't do:** Theo doesn't price anything (that's Maya) and he doesn't run
the job once it's underway (that's the Project Coordinator). His job is getting the
sequence legal and realistic, not tracking daily progress.

**How he checks himself:** A construction job has a legal build order. You can't
frame a new window opening *after* the wall is already drywalled. Theo's schedule
gets graded against that hard order (the "schedule oracle"). If he sequences framing
after drywall, the grader flags the violation, Theo moves framing back before
rough-in and inspection, and re-checks. You see the corrected, buildable schedule and
a note that he fixed the order.

---

### 4. The Project Coordinator: runs the job during construction

**Name:** Rosa

**What Rosa does:** Once the job is live, Rosa is on it. She handles RFIs (the
"requests for information" that come up when something in the field doesn't match the
plans), tracks progress against the schedule, and watches for margin leaks: the small
overruns and missed change orders that quietly eat your profit. When a homeowner asks
for something extra mid-job, Rosa is usually the first to catch that it needs to
become a priced change.

**What Rosa won't do:** Rosa doesn't set the original price or build the original
schedule (that's pre-construction work for Maya and Theo). She watches the live job
and raises the flag. When a change needs pricing or rescheduling, she routes it
through Sam to the right specialist instead of doing it herself.

**How she fits:** Rosa is your early-warning system. She spots the change or the leak,
names it, and kicks off the hand-off that gets it priced, scheduled, and papered.

---

### 5. The Office Admin: the paperwork

**Name:** Dev

**What Dev does:** Dev produces the documents. Change orders, invoices, and reminders
all come off Dev's desk. When the Estimator has a final number and the Scheduler has
the added days, Dev writes up Change Order #001 with the cost and the schedule impact,
updates the invoice, and sets a reminder: "CO #001 needs owner signature." Dev is the
one who makes sure nothing falls through the cracks.

**What Dev won't do:** Dev doesn't invent prices or schedules. Dev assembles the
paperwork from the numbers the specialists already produced and verified. Dev also
never sends money or signs on your behalf. The reminder goes to *you*; the signature
comes from the owner.

**How Dev fits:** Dev is the last stop before a document reaches a human. By the time
Dev writes the change order, the price has been graded and the schedule has been
graded. Dev just packages the verified work into something you can sign.

## How a change order moves through the office

Here's the whole flow in one walk-through. Say a kitchen remodel is mid-construction
and the homeowner decides they want to add an egress window.

1. **You tell the Office Manager.** "Homeowner wants an egress window in the kitchen."
   Sam takes the request. You're done talking to anyone but Sam.

2. **The Coordinator catches it as a change.** Rosa is running the live job. She
   recognizes this isn't in the original scope. It needs to become a priced change
   order, not a freebie. She raises it and hands it back to Sam.

3. **Sam routes it to the Estimator.** Maya prices the new work: framing the rough
   opening, the window unit, flashing, the drywall patch, trim, paint, a siding
   patch. On her first pass she double-counts the framing labor. The estimate grader
   catches it, she repairs the math, and the corrected delta lands at about $1,850.

4. **Sam routes it to the Scheduler.** Theo slots the added work into the calendar.
   His first pass schedules the window framing *after* the drywall, which is illegal.
   The schedule grader flags the trade-order violation, Theo moves framing back to
   where it belongs, and the corrected schedule adds about 2 days.

5. **Sam routes it to the Office Admin.** Dev writes Change Order #001: +$1,850,
   +2 days. Dev updates the invoice and sets a reminder that the change order needs
   the owner's signature.

6. **The owner signs.** The finished change order comes back through Sam to you, and
   the homeowner signs it. Money only moves after a human says yes.

That's the office at work: one request in, a verified change order out, every number
checked against a hard rule before it reached a person, and you only ever talked to
one digital employee the whole time.
